Letter from an Unknown Woman

By Marc Lee

6:00PM BST 21 Apr 2014

James Mason, who appeared in two of Max Ophüls's films, once wrote a short poem about the director that begins: "A shot that does not call for tracks / Is agony for poor dear Max / Who separated from his dolly / Is wrapped in deepest melancholy." The luscious visual audacity of Letter from an Unknown Woman makes Mason's case perfectly.

Swift, swooping, dizzying, Max Ophüls’ shooting style matches brilliantly the vertiginous melodrama of his story. The lighting, too, intensifies the agonies of a doomed romance, as well as subtly accentuating the lovers' innocence and guilt: she is bathed in a beatific glow; he is forever in dubious shadow.

Set a century ago in Vienna, the story (based on the novella of the same name by Stefan Zweig) opens with a terrific grab at the heartstrings. The night before he is due to fight a duel, philandering concert pianist Stefan Brand (Louis Jourdan) receives a letter.

"By the time you read this," it begins, "I may be dead." The writer is Lisa Berndle (Joan Fontaine), with whom Stefan had a fling 10 years earlier. In a series of flashbacks, the story of her obsession with him (which began when she was a child living next door) is revealed.

Cruelly, Lisa's passion is unrequited; even worse, Stefan – befuddled by a lifetime's womanizing – has no idea who she is when she turns up at his apartment.

Weak-willed, selfish, exploitative, Stefan is certainly the villain of the piece. But Lisa's letter (which contains more than one bombshell) brings about a transformation, and, as dawn approaches, he heads towards his uncertain fate with newfound courage.

It is a heartbreaking yet strangely uplifting moment.
